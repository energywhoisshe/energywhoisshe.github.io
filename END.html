I invite into this space bodies still/yet/unknowingly here and a bouquet of flowers bursting with life like a MAH-UHM beyond time [1]. With drafting paper, dak paper, mulberry paper, backing paper, and photographic paper, I prepared places from which to suspend these flowers. I recited Eon Hee, read Theresa, and installed an underground tunnel.

February is a month of graduations. Both graduate schools and kindergartens hold graduation ceremonies. Flowers suck in water with their bodies cut at the root, as their passageways turn to pulp. The poet smells poetry from a bouquet of flowers. The dog barks early like an alarm clock every morning. The bed stands on all fours. The door, with its four corners, opens and closes. The bright yellow light from the window also has four corners to its shadow, but it falls diagonally as if italicized. Snow falls in spring, too.

I first examined postmortem changes and damages, then specifically neonatal and pediatric deaths as well as deaths from sex crimes. Deaths from choking, drowning, addiction, and starvation followed. Even as I committed certain chapters to memory, small details would still appear from secluded windows. Some bodies I drew multiple times. Initially, I worried this was not fair to the others, but befriending someone means that their face draws closer than other faces. Given the small size of reference images in forensic books, I discovered new details each time I drew them. For example, what I thought was flesh turned out to be an eye, and a spine was in fact an umbilical cord. I felt a strange obligation to draw every page. As I obsessively took dictation of their deaths at all hours, sleep turned from a time of rest to that of spiritual connection. A deaf, blind white dog came to my doorstep, and as soon as I decided to take him under my care, I stopped suffering from sleep paralysis. The white fur of an orphaned albino boy begins to occupy my home. He naps emitting a white light that shines even in the pitch-dark corner of my studio.

Not long ago, the reservoir of my body [2] overflowed. I have witnessed the deaths of my father, my grandfather, a dog, a sparrow, a pigeon, a snake, and a mouse, in addition to the deaths of countless cows, pigs, chicken, insects, trees, and flowers. Given how I can visualize accidents overheard on the taxi radio (as if Iâ€™ve seen them firsthand), this practice of taking dictation from open bodies seemingly functions as both a rumination and a rehearsal for all the deaths I have witnessed and will witness throughout my life. These days I imagine passersby in two scenarios: first, the stranger having met their end, and second, the stranger writing poetry with an unknowable expression.
